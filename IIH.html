Interactive Summary: Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension (IIH)

Based on: Yiangou A, Mollan SP, Sinclair AJ. Idiopathic intracranial hypertension: a step change in understanding the disease mechanisms. Nature Reviews Neurology. 2023;19:769-785.

Key Insights from the Review

  • IIH is evolving from a neuro-ophthalmic disorder to a probable systemic metabolic disease.
  • Features now include impaired fertility, gestational diabetes, pre-eclampsia, increased cardiometabolic risk, and cognitive dysfunction.
  • Comorbidities: PCOS, OSA, anxiety, depression.
  • Headache and ICP relationship is complex; specific signalling pathways (e.g., CGRP) are implicated.
  • Weight loss is the only established disease-modifying therapy; bariatric surgery shows sustained ICP control.
  • New metabolic therapies are emerging, e.g., GLP-1 receptor agonists (exenatide) significantly reduce ICP.
  • Adipose tissue dysfunction (priming for lipogenesis, centripetal distribution) and unique androgen excess promoting CSF secretion are key mechanistic insights.

Introduction & Epidemiology

IIH, previously seen as mainly neuro-ophthalmic (raised ICP, headache, papilloedema, visual loss risk), is now being redefined as a probable metabolic disease with systemic manifestations. Over 95% affected are reproductive-age women with obesity. Incidence is rising with global obesity rates.

  • Pooled Incidence (1988-2017): 1.2/100,000 per year (North America, Africa, Europe, Asia).
  • Recent Rise in Developed Countries:
    • England (2016): 350% increase over 10 years (4.7/100,000 per year).
    • Wales (2017): Prevalence 76/100,000; Incidence 7.8/100,000 per year.
    • UK Women (2017): Prevalence 79/100,000; Incidence 9.3/100,000 per year.
    • Highest in women aged 20-29 years (16.5/100,000 per year).
  • Correlation with Obesity: Country-specific IIH incidence correlates with national obesity rates.
  • Social Deprivation: Higher incidence in areas of social deprivation in the UK.
  • Public Health Concern: Rising incidence, hospital admissions, and financial burden suggest IIH needs population-targeted prevention.

Disease Features: Classical and Emergent

  • Papilloedema: Can lead to visual loss.
    • Historically (1982): Severe/permanent visual loss in up to 25%.
    • More recently (2013): 0.6-2% fully sight impaired. (Difference may be due to definition of visual loss).
  • Other Visual Features: Transient visual obscurations (68%), perceived visual loss (32%), double vision (18%).
  • Most Common Symptom: Headache (>80%).
  • Other Symptoms: Back pain (53%), pulsatile tinnitus (52%), dizziness (52%), neck/radicular pain.
  • IIH without Papilloedema (IIHWOP): Subclassification; cause of refractory headaches, visual/neurological disability. Potentially under-represented. Therapeutic suitability unclear.

Box 1: Emergent Features of IIH

  • Impairment associated with ICP and influences visual field performance.
  • Management: ICP reduction can improve cognition. Address modifiable factors (headache, mental health, OSA, medications).
  • Studies confirm impairments in multiple domains (attention, visual-spatial, global scores). Persists at follow-up.
  • Confounders: headache, BMI, mental health.
  • Cognitive performance influenced by headache severity, depression, OSA.
  • Association with neurophysiological tests (P300, contingent negative variation).
  • Low fertility in people with IIH.
  • Increased pregnancy complications vs. general population:
    • Pre-eclampsia (5.3-fold)
    • Gestational diabetes (2.7-fold)
  • Increased elective caesarean delivery rates (2.4-fold).
  • Management: May require specialist obstetric input. Enhanced monitoring during pregnancy. Weight optimization and disease control pre-pregnancy recommended. Vaginal delivery generally safe.
  • 1.5-fold increased risk in people with IIH vs. general population.
  • Shared phenotype: reproductive-age women with obesity.
  • Management: May require specific management and specialist input for weight loss. Important for fertility management.
  • High prevalence in IIH (33-63%). One study found 47% had OSA.
  • Management: Appropriate screening (e.g., STOP-BANG, which has 84% sensitivity in IIH). Referral to specialist services. Treatment can improve papilloedema. Overnight oximetry also used (49% yield).
  • Higher prevalence of depression and anxiety vs. general population. Similar prevalence to migraine sufferers.
  • Psychiatric comorbidities (45%) associated with worse outcomes.
  • Obesity not sole determinant; headache, migraine, BMI are risk factors.
  • Management: Screen for depression/anxiety, signpost management. Address modifiable risk factors (headache, medications).

Advances in Disease Monitoring

Mainstay of clinical practice for papilloedema management. Allows quantitative measures of optic nerve head (ONH) height/volume and qualitative anatomical findings.

  • Differentiation: Helps distinguish papilloedema from pseudopapilloedema (e.g., ONH drusen, peripapillary wrinkles).
  • Prognosis: Can reveal intraretinal folds/fluid indicating poor prognosis if untreated. (Referenced Fig 1, 2, 3 in article for RNFL progression, disc volume assessment, and fluid).
  • Correlation with ICP: OCT measures (e.g., central thickness) correlate with ICP, acting as a surrogate measure.
  • Functional Correlation: Macular ganglion cell layer volume correlates with visual field mean deviation, predicting functional disability.
  • Advantages over Visual Fields: Visual fields can be unreliable due to patient factors (cognitive deficits, symptoms, learning effects). OCT is more objective.
  • Relapse Detection: Effective in identifying recurrent papilloedema (increased retinal nerve fibre layer thickness) even with optic atrophy.

Increased use in neurosurgery over past 5-10 years. Allows long-term, repeated ICP measurement.

  • Use Cases: Complex IIH, post-shunt placement for monitoring and adjustment, suspected shunt failure, determining if headaches are due to high/low ICP.
  • ICP Physiology Insights:
    • No diurnal ICP variation in active IIH with papilloedema.
    • ICP increases with duration of supine position.
    • ICP falls by ~51% when moving from lying to standing/sitting.
    • Mean ICP rose 34% during prolonged supine position (may explain worse morning headaches/T VOs).
    • Brief supine/standing measurements can predict prolonged daytime ICP.
  • Waveform Analysis: Pulse amplitude can indicate intracranial compliance.
  • Future Research: Needed for feasibility, safety, efficacy over longer periods in normal daily activities.
  • Accurate phenotyping (ICHD-3 criteria) is essential.
  • Tools:
    • Headache diaries: Frequency, severity, analgesic use.
    • Self-reported assessments: Headache Impact Test-6 (HIT-6), Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS).
    • Quality of life measures: Medical Outcomes Study 36-item short form (SF-36).

IIH as a Systemic Metabolic Disease

Evidence points to IIH as a disease of altered CSF regulation with strong metabolic and hormonal underpinnings, rather than purely idiopathic.

Box 2: IIH Emerging as a Systemic Metabolic Disease - Key Findings & Impact

  • Androgens: Unique Profile
    • Unique hormonal signature of androgen excess (elevated testosterone in serum & CSF).
    • Testosterone increases CSF production in vitro and in vivo (rats).
    • Elevated 5α-reductase activity. Markers of hypothalamic-pituitary axis activation.
  • Glucocorticoids: Dysregulation
    • Dysregulation with higher activity of 5α-reductase and 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 1 (11β-HSD1) in urine and adipose tissue.
    • 11β-HSD1 (converts cortisone to cortisol) found in choroid plexus, influences CSF cortisol.
    • Inhibition of 11β-HSD1 may be associated with ICP reduction.
  • Perturbed Metabolism (Metabolomics): Altered Pathways
    • Altered amino acid pathways (arginine, proline, histidine).
    • Altered acylpyruvates (formylpyruvate, maleylpyruvate-fumarylpyruvate) - lower in CSF, higher in serum.
    • Altered lipid profile (glycerophospholipids, ceramides) that correlates with ICP.
    • Altered CSF-to-serum urea gradient. Raised CSF acetate concentrations.
  • Insulin & Adipose Tissue: Dysfunction
    • Increased insulin resistance (correlates with ICP and centripetal adiposity).
    • Truncal/centripetal fat distribution (correlates with ICP).
    • Increased leptin secretion.
    • Adipocytes transcriptionally primed for lipogenesis and weight gain; altered lipid metabolic pathways.
  • Cardiovascular Risk: Twofold Increased Twofold increased risk of cardiovascular events (heart failure, ischemic heart disease, stroke/TIA) vs. matched controls, independent of obesity. 55% increased hypertension risk, 30% increased type 2 diabetes risk.
  • Reproductive Health: Adverse Outcomes Lower live birth rate, increased risk of pre-eclampsia and gestational diabetes.
  • Metabolic Health: Compromised Increased risk of type 2 diabetes and gestational diabetes.
  • Weight Loss Benefit: Therapeutic Link Bariatric surgery reduces ICP and is associated with increased GLP-1 secretion in line with ICP reduction.
  • CSF Production: Likely elevated in IIH (pilot study: mean 86 ml/h vs. normal 18-24 ml/h). Mainly at choroid plexus via Na+/K+ ATPase.
  • Potential Targets: Channels/receptors in CSF dynamics (aquaporins, Na-K-Cl cotransporter, GLUT1, TRVP4).
  • Glymphatic System: Growing evidence for involvement of elevated cerebral venous sinus pressure and dysregulation of the glymphatic system (transports fluid via peri-arterial channels, perivenous spaces, lymphatics). Dysfunction of aquaporins, pro-inflammatory profile, neurogliovascular unit disruption proposed.

IIH Headache & Targeted Management

  • Predominant disabling symptom, drives poor quality of life.
  • Frequency: >80% report headache. 3-fold increased hazard for new headache, >2-fold for new migraine vs. women without IIH.
  • Phenotype: Often resembles migraine (pressure 47%, throbbing 42%). Associated features: nausea (47%), vomiting (17%), photophobia (70%), phonophobia (52%), exacerbation on physical activity (50%).
  • Persistent Post-IIH Headache: Can occur after papilloedema resolution, often chronic migraine-like.
  • Medication Challenges: High opiate prescription rates (2x migraine controls). More preventative medication use than migraine controls. Medication overuse common (≥37%).
  • Mechanisms:
    • ICP Relationship: Debated. Acute ICP lowering (LP) improves headache in 71-72%. Bariatric surgery (reduces ICP) improves headache. However, 11β-HSD1 inhibitor RCT (reduced ICP) didn't improve chronic headache. Acetazolamide RCT (reduced ICP) no headache improvement. Relationship complex, likely changes over disease course.
    • Central Sensitization: Suggested by migraine-like phenotype and correlation of ICP with cutaneous allodynia.
    • Trigeminovascular System & CGRP: CGRP implicated. Blocking CGRP (erenumab) prevented raised ICP headache in a case series and showed benefit in a prospective open-label study for persistent post-IIH headache.
    • Metabolomic Links: Altered CSF-serum urea gradient and raised CSF acetate correlate with headache severity.

Box 3: Principles of IIH Management

  • Treat Underlying Disease with Weight Loss: Disease-Modifying
    • Bariatric surgery is evidenced for long-term ICP control. Average 24% weight loss associated with disease remission (ICP <25 cmCSF).
  • Protect Vision: Critical
    • Prompt surgical options for immediate threat to vision.
    • Medical approaches if no immediate vision threat.
  • Reduce Headache Burden: Symptomatic Relief
    • Target underlying phenotype.
    • Cautious use of headache therapies (consider weight gain, mood effects).
  • Calorie Restriction: Prospective studies show improved clinical outcomes.
  • Bariatric Surgery: RCT showed it's disease-modifying for long-term ICP control vs. community weight management. Significant ICP lowering at 12 & 24 months, more weight loss, better QoL. ICP improvements seen as early as 2 weeks post-op (gut hormone changes?). Cost-effective. Access varies.
  • Licensed Therapies: None.
  • Acetazolamide: Most commonly prescribed (58% in UK first year). IIHTT (USA): modest visual field improvement with low-sodium diet vs. diet alone. Target 4g/day (mean 2.5g achieved). Poorly tolerated (48% withdrawal in smaller RCT). Use is physician-dependent.
  • Other Diuretics (Topiramate etc.): Off-label. Topiramate prescribed in 20% (UK). Open-label study exists, but no placebo control. Reduces ICP more than acetazolamide in healthy rats. Furosemide/amiloride data limited/old.
  • IIH Headache Management:
    • Therapeutic LPs: Minimal efficacy for headache, risk of exacerbation, traumatizing. Not recommended.
    • Weight loss (low-calorie diet): Significantly improves headache in newly diagnosed IIH. Bariatric surgery trial didn't show significant headache reduction (not primary endpoint).
    • Migraine preventatives (topiramate, candesartan, onabotulinum toxin A): Off-label, no evidence for IIH, choose carefully (avoid weight gain/mood worsening).
    • Acute migraine-like attacks: Triptans + simple analgesic + prokinetic anti-emetic.
    • Medication Overuse: Educate early.
    • CGRP Blockade (e.g., Erenumab): Open-label study showed significant reduction in headache burden at 12 months for persistent post-IIH headache. Retrospective review less conclusive. Caution: may mask IIH relapse. Regular fundoscopy recommended.
  • Potential Novel Targeted Management:
    • 11β-HSD1 Inhibitor (AZD4017): Phase II RCT: significantly reduced ICP vs. baseline (by 4.3 cmCSF), but primary outcome (vs. placebo) not met. Well tolerated. Improved lipid profiles, increased lean muscle mass. Not evaluated further in IIH.
    • GLP-1 Receptor Agonists:
      • GLP1Rs in choroid plexus. Exenatide modulates CSF secretion in vitro, reduces ICP in rats (45-55%).
      • Exenatide: Phase II RCT (telemetric ICP monitors): significant ICP reduction vs. placebo at 2.5h, 24h, 12 weeks (by 5.7, 6.4, 5.6 cmCSF respectively). Paved way for Phase III trial of once-weekly exenatide (Presendin, NCT05347147).
      • Liraglutide/Semaglutide: Open-label case-control: more effective for weight loss and headache frequency vs. usual care. No ICP/visual data.
  • For sight-threatening disease. Consensus on timing/type lacking internationally.
  • CSF Diversion (Shunts): Well-established effectiveness. High failure/complication rates (30-50% revision in 1st year). Characterized cohort needing shunts: mean LP pressure 45 cmCSF, Frisén grade ≥4, VF mean deviation -11 dB.
  • Optic Nerve Sheath Fenestration (ONSF): Effective for vision preservation in refractory IIH. Complications include optic nerve fibrosis, need for revision.
  • Venous Sinus Stenting: Growing supporting evidence, but lack of RCTs/long-term data prevents routine use.
  • Trial assessing surgical interventions terminated due to poor recruitment. New trial (shunting vs. stenting) enrolling (ISRCTN57142415).

Conclusion & Future Directions

  • Substantial progress in understanding IIH mechanisms: no longer "idiopathic" but related to systemic metabolic and hormonal perturbations.
  • Key features: abnormal metabolome, unique androgen signature, dysregulated glucocorticoids, insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes risk, twofold increased cardiovascular risk, centripetal adiposity, adipose tissue primed for lipogenesis.
  • Weight loss is the only established disease-modifying therapy; bariatric surgery offers long-term ICP control.
  • Clinical spectrum: impaired fertility, pregnancy complications, cognitive dysfunction, OSA, adverse mental health. Clinicians should address these.
  • Management: Requires multidisciplinary approach (endocrinology lead suggested). Specialist weight services crucial.
  • Therapeutic Pipeline: Targeting GLP1R (exenatide) is a promising avenue. CGRP identified as a target for IIH headache.
  • Disease Monitoring: OCT is key for quantifying papilloedema and as ICP surrogate, superior to visual fields for objective monitoring. Telemetric ICP monitoring aids complex disease management.
  • Future Redefinition: Given evidence, "metabolic intracranial hypertension" might be a more appropriate term.
  • Need: Carefully planned therapeutic pipelines and clinical trials to deliver targeted therapeutics.
