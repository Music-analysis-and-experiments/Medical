Trigeminal Postherpetic Neuralgia (TG-PHN): Summary

Interactive summary based on Niemeyer et al., Current Pain and Headache Reports (2024) 28:295-306

Overview & Introduction

Definition: TG-PHN is a neuropathic pain condition resulting from herpes zoster (HZ, shingles) affecting the trigeminal nerve (CN V). Pain persists or recurs for at least 3 months after the HZ rash onset.

Cause: Reactivation of the Varicella Zoster Virus (VZV), which remains latent in trigeminal ganglion (TG) neurons after primary infection (chickenpox).

Epidemiology:

  • HZ affects >1 million annually in the USA.
  • Most cephalic (head/face) zoster (58%) involves the trigeminal nerve.
  • The first division (V1, ophthalmic) is most common, termed Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus (HZO), accounting for 2.5-20% of all HZ cases.
  • TG-PHN risk is higher compared to PHN in other body locations.
  • Affects quality of life severely and can persist for months, years, or indefinitely (22-46% > 2 years).

Purpose of Review: To explore clinical characteristics, pathophysiology, and treatment options for TG-PHN.

Risk Factors for Developing PHN after HZ

  • Advancing Age: Incidence dramatically increases with age (risk >40% in HZ cases over 60). 80 years is a noted cutoff for higher risk after HZO.
  • Location: Trigeminal nerve involvement (especially V1/HZO) carries >2x risk compared to non-ophthalmic zoster.
  • Rash Severity: Severe HZ skin eruption increases PHN risk. Eye involvement (keratitis, uveitis) may also increase risk. Suggests higher viral load/inflammation.
  • Pain Severity (Acute HZ): Severe pain during the initial HZ episode is strongly associated with developing PHN.
  • Zoster Sine Herpete (ZSH): Zoster occurring without a rash. Associated pain can be more intense and prolonged than typical HZ/PHN.
  • Demographics: Higher frequency noted in women and White individuals compared to men and other races/ethnicities.
  • Comorbidities/Other: Potential links to persistent VZV DNA in blood cells and increased stroke risk post-HZ (especially HZO) warrant further study.

Clinical Features of TG-PHN

  • Pain: Highly variable qualities:
    • Deep, burning, aching (continuous)
    • Lancinating, shooting, stabbing (paroxysmal)
    • Often a combination
  • Sensory Disturbances: Common alongside pain:
    • Allodynia: Pain evoked by normally non-painful stimuli (e.g., light touch, breeze) - indicates central sensitization.
    • Hyperalgesia: Increased pain from painful stimuli.
    • Paresthesia/Dysesthesia: Abnormal sensations (tingling, pins & needles) / unpleasant abnormal sensations.
    • Numbness: Sensory loss, often more prominent in TG-PHN vs truncal PHN, suggesting more nerve degeneration.
  • Postherpetic Pruritus (PHP): Chronic itching. More frequent in TG-PHN (43%) than other locations (25%). Can be severe and debilitating.
  • Trigeminal Trophic Syndrome: Skin/tissue damage (ulceration) in the affected area, can occur months/years later, sometimes due to nerve damage or even treatment complications.
  • Systemic/Cerebral Symptoms: High prevalence (~40%) of insomnia, anxiety, depression, cognitive difficulties, chronic fatigue, weight loss, anorexia.
  • Physical Exam & Testing:
    • Significant sensory changes (loss or altered sensation) on the affected side compared to the unaffected side, even in adjacent branches.
    • Neurophysiological tests may show abnormalities (e.g., delayed blink reflex).
    • Imaging (CT/MRI) is usually normal but can rarely show TG enhancement (ganglionitis) or brainstem signal changes ("trigeminal pontine sign"). Research MRI shows CNS structural/functional changes (e.g., thalamic volume, connectivity).

4. Central Nervous System - Thalamus (VPM) & Cortex

  • Pain signals ascend from the brainstem (SNVc) to the Ventral Posteromedial (VPM) nucleus of the thalamus.
  • Altered Thalamic Processing: Evidence suggests decreased GABAergic inhibition within the thalamus (in animal models), leading to amplified pain signals relayed to the somatosensory cortex.
  • Cortical Changes: Structural (e.g., cortical thickness changes) and functional alterations in brain areas related to sensory, motor, and cognitive/emotional aspects of pain contribute to the overall experience and persistence of TG-PHN. Altered connectivity in pain modulation pathways (e.g., involving periaqueductal gray).

Treatment of TG-PHN

Management is challenging; prevention is key. Early treatment (< 9 months) may yield better outcomes.

Prevention

  • Vaccination:
    • RZV (Shingrix): Recombinant subunit vaccine. Preferred. High efficacy against HZ and PHN (76-88%), including in older adults. Requires 2 doses. Can be reactogenic (local soreness, fatigue).
    • ZVL (Zostavax): Live attenuated vaccine. Less effective, efficacy wanes, not for immunocompromised.
  • Early HZ Treatment: Antivirals, analgesics during acute HZ. Evidence for preventing PHN is weak.
  • Interventional (Prevention?): Pulsed radiofrequency (PRF) of Gasserian ganglion during acute HZ showed promise in one study for preventing PHN in elderly. More research needed.

Conservative: Systemic Medications

  • Gabapentinoids: First Line. Modulate α2δ subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels.
    • Pregabalin (Lyrica): 300-600 mg/day. FDA Approved. May be better for pain/sleep than gabapentin.
    • Gabapentin (Neurontin): 1800-3600 mg/day. FDA Approved. May be better tolerated than pregabalin.
    • Mirogabalin: 15-30 mg/day. Newer α2δ ligand, showed efficacy in PHN study (including TG-PHN).
    Side effects: dizziness, somnolence, edema. Titrate slowly.
  • Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs): First Line (Off-label). Inhibit serotonin/norepinephrine reuptake, block channels.
    • Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline: 50-150 mg/day (often lower doses tolerated).
    Significant side effects, especially in elderly (anticholinergic, cardiac). Use with caution. Nortriptyline often better tolerated.
  • Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs): Off-label.
    • Duloxetine (Cymbalta): 60-120 mg/day.
    • Venlafaxine (Effexor): 150-225 mg/day.
    May be better tolerated than TCAs. Side effects: nausea, insomnia, BP changes (Venlafaxine).
  • Other Antiseizure Medications: Off-label. Based on benefit in other neuropathic pains (like trigeminal neuralgia).
    • Carbamazepine, Oxcarbazepine, Lamotrigine, Valproic Acid.
    Variable efficacy in PHN, significant side effect profiles, monitoring required.
  • Opioids:
    • Tramadol, others.
    Frequently prescribed but NOT first-line. Limited long-term efficacy, risk of tolerance, dependence, side effects (constipation, sedation). Consider only if other options fail/insufficient.
  • Combination Therapy: Often needed when monotherapy is insufficient or limited by side effects.

Conservative: Local/Topical Treatments

  • Lidocaine 5% Patch (Lidoderm): FDA Approved. Blocks voltage-gated sodium channels locally. Apply up to 12 hours/day. Targeted relief, minimal systemic absorption. Skin irritation possible.
  • Capsaicin 8% Patch (Qutenza): FDA Approved. High-concentration TRPV1 agonist, causes initial burning then desensitization. Single application every 3 months in clinic. Reportedly used for TG-PHN but requires caution due to facial application/irritation. Pre-treatment with local anesthetic needed.
  • Low-dose Capsaicin Cream (0.025-0.075%): Evidence is inconclusive. Requires multiple daily applications, burning sensation common.

Conservative: Non-Pharmacologic

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): May help improve coping, reduce impact of pain, address associated anxiety/depression. Evidence specifically for PHN is limited but suggested.
  • Acupuncture: May reduce pain intensity and anxiety. Limited high-quality evidence, variable results.

Interventional Treatments (for Refractory Pain)

  • Botulinum Toxin-A (BTX-A) Injections: Subdermal injections in the painful area. Trials show significant pain reduction vs placebo. Mechanism complex (sensory nerve terminal effects). Side effects: local pain, potential facial muscle weakness (transient). Optimal dosing/standardization ongoing.
  • Nerve Blocks: Injection of local anesthetic (+/- steroid) around specific trigeminal branches (e.g., supraorbital, infraorbital). Can provide diagnostic information and temporary relief. Repeated blocks may be needed. Steroid risks (atrophy).
  • Nerve Ablation (Radiofrequency/Cryoablation): Considered if diagnostic blocks are successful. Destroys nerve fibers for longer-lasting relief. Risk of permanent numbness, anesthesia dolorosa (painful numbness), skin burns (esp. with continuous RFA). Pulsed RFA may be safer.
  • Stellate Ganglion Block (SGB): Blockade of sympathetic ganglion in the neck. More commonly used for acute HZ pain. Variable and often limited efficacy reported for chronic TG-PHN. Risks: Horner syndrome (usually transient), phrenic nerve palsy, vascular injury.
  • Spinal Cord Stimulation (SCS): Implantation of electrodes near the high cervical spinal cord / cervicomedullary junction to modulate pain signals in the SNVc. Emerging technique for TG-PHN. Technically demanding. Potential for paresthesia coverage issues, hardware complications. Promising results in small series (>70% relief >1 yr).
  • Peripheral Nerve Stimulation (PNS): Electrodes placed directly adjacent to affected trigeminal branches (e.g., supraorbital, infraorbital). Less invasive than SCS. Good option for V1/V2 pain. Requires successful trial period. Coverage for V3 (mandibular) can be difficult. Reports show good outcomes in small series (reduced pain/opioid use). Hardware complications possible.

Note: Target doses are often limited by tolerability. Many treatments are used off-label for TG-PHN.

Conclusion

TG-PHN is a common, complex, and debilitating condition arising from VZV reactivation in the trigeminal nerve.

Its pathophysiology involves a cascade of events from peripheral inflammation and sensitization to central changes in the brainstem, thalamus, and cortex, involving numerous molecular mediators and altered neuronal excitability/inhibition.

Current management relies on prevention (vaccination), pharmacological therapies (gabapentinoids, TCAs often first-line), topical agents, and interventional approaches for refractory cases.

Further research into the intricate peripheral and central mechanisms is crucial for developing more targeted and effective therapies to improve outcomes for patients suffering from TG-PHN.
