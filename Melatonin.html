Interactive Summary: Melatonin in Sleep Disorders

Based on: Poza JJ, Pujol M, Ortega-Albás JJ, Romero O. Melatonina en los trastornos de sueño. Neurología. 2022;37:575-585.
(Review article, received April 2018, accepted August 2018)

Introduction & Physiology of Melatonin

Melatonin (N-acetyl-5-methoxytryptamine) is the primary hormone regulating the sleep-wake cycle. Its potential use in treating insomnia is significant due to its natural role, oral availability, and age-related decline potentially linking it to sleep quality issues in older adults.

Synthesis Pathway:

Tryptophan → Serotonin → N-acetylserotonin → Melatonin

Regulation:

  • Primarily synthesized in the pineal gland, but also in other tissues (retina, gut, immune cells).
  • Regulated by the Suprachiasmatic Nucleus (SCN), the body's master clock.
  • Light (especially blue light detected by melanopsin in retinal ganglion cells) inhibits melatonin production via the retinohypothalamic tract.
  • Production increases in darkness, typically starting around 8-10 PM, peaking between midnight and 3 AM.

Lifespan Changes:

  • Production starts at 3-4 months of age.
  • Peaks during childhood (ages 8-10).
  • Decreases dramatically during puberty.
  • Progressively decreases after age 40-45; by age 70, levels are ~10% of prepubertal levels. Link to Ageing

Daily Cycle (Circadian Rhythm):

  • Synthesized in response to darkness.
  • Peak levels (100-200 pg/mL) occur at night, usually when core body temperature is lowest.
  • Daytime levels are very low (10-30 pg/mL).
  • Morning light exposure advances the cycle (earlier peak); evening light delays it.

Primary Role:

  • Chronobiotic: Signals "darkness" to the body, synchronizing internal clocks and promoting sleep. Acts on SCN and peripheral clocks.
  • Thermoregulation: Contributes to the drop in body temperature associated with sleep onset.

Other Potential Roles:

  • Antioxidant: Direct scavenger of reactive oxygen/nitrogen species; stimulates antioxidant enzymes.
  • Immune Modulation: Generally immunostimulatory.
  • Oncostatic Effects: Potential role in cancer prevention/treatment (experimental evidence, clinical trials ongoing).
  • Reproductive Cycle Regulation: Involved in timing of puberty and seasonal reproduction in animals.

Pharmacological Properties

Absorption & Distribution:

  • Rapidly absorbed orally.
  • Maximum concentration reached in ~40 mins (immediate-release). Food delays absorption.
  • Low Bioavailability (3-33%): Due to significant first-pass metabolism in the liver.
  • Freely crosses the blood-brain barrier.

Metabolism & Excretion:

  • Mainly metabolized in the liver by CYP1A2 enzyme (6-hydroxylation), followed by conjugation (sulfation/glucuronidation).
  • Metabolites excreted in urine (main one: 6-sulfatoxymelatonin).
  • Half-life: Short (~45-65 mins for immediate-release).

Influencing Factors:

  • Caffeine (CYP1A2 inhibitor): Increases bioavailability and half-life.
  • Smoking (CYP1A2 inducer): Decreases bioavailability.
  • Fluvoxamine (CYP1A2 inhibitor): Increases half-life.
  • Children have faster clearance than adults. No major sex differences noted.

Receptors:

  • Binds primarily to two G-protein coupled receptors:
    • MT1: Hypothesized to be linked to hypnotic (sleep-inducing) effects.
    • MT2: Hypothesized to be linked to circadian phase shifting and pain modulation.
  • A third site, MT3 (enzyme quinone reductase 2), has been identified, but its role is less clear.
  • Receptors are found in the SCN, other brain areas, and peripheral tissues.
  • Binding inhibits adenylate cyclase, affecting gene expression related to circadian rhythms.

Receptor Sensitivity:

  • High concentrations (like from immediate-release spikes) can cause receptor desensitization/internalization in experimental models.
  • Physiological levels (mimicked by prolonged-release) may avoid this issue.

Immediate-Release (IR):

  • Rapid absorption, peak in ~40 mins.
  • Short half-life (~1 hour).
  • Levels drop quickly, potentially before natural melatonin would still be high.
  • May be better suited for sleep-onset difficulties.
  • Creates high peak concentrations, potentially causing receptor desensitization.

Prolonged-Release (PR) (e.g., Circadin®):

  • Slower absorption, delayed peak (90-210 mins).
  • Longer half-life (~3.5-4 hours).
  • Maintains stable plasma concentrations for 8-10 hours.
  • More closely mimics the natural physiological secretion pattern.
  • May be better suited for sleep maintenance difficulties.
  • Circadin® specifically approved by EMA for primary insomnia in adults >55 years.

Melatonin for Insomnia Treatment

Insomnia is common (19-24%). Treatment should start with behavioral therapy.

  • First-line pharmacotherapy: Melatonin is generally considered the first choice when medication is needed. Recommended
  • Indication: Primarily for sleep-onset insomnia.
  • Dosage (Typical):
    • Infants/Preschool: 1-3 mg
    • School-age: 2.5-5 mg
    • Adolescents: 1-5 mg
    • Start low, administer 30-60 mins before bedtime. Limit use to <4 weeks.
  • Delayed Sleep Phase Syndrome (DSPS): Melatonin effective, especially low doses (e.g., 1mg) administered 3-5 hours before dim light melatonin onset (DLMO). Effective for DSPS
  • Formulation: Data mostly on immediate-release. No evidence PR is superior here.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) is the first-line treatment.

  • General Recommendation (European Guideline 2017): Insufficient evidence to broadly recommend melatonin (IR or PR) for primary insomnia in adults <55 yrs. Insufficient Evidence
  • Evidence Summary:
    • Meta-analyses show small, statistically significant decreases in sleep onset latency (time to fall asleep). Clinical significance debated.
    • Effects on sleep quality are inconsistent or small.
    • Safety profile appears good.
  • Adults >55 years: Prolonged-Release Melatonin (Circadin® 2mg) is approved and recommended, particularly for sleep maintenance/quality issues. Recommended >55 (PR)
  • Depression/Anxiety: Evidence for improving mood is lacking. PR melatonin (2.5-10mg) as adjunct to fluoxetine showed improved sleep and mood compared to placebo. May improve insomnia secondary to bipolar disorder. Mixed/Adjunct
  • Schizophrenia: Small studies suggest IR (3mg) or PR (2mg) can improve sleep onset, reduce awakenings, and increase sleep duration. Mixed
  • Intellectual Disability / Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD): Sleep disorders common, often linked to altered melatonin production. Melatonin (variable doses, IR/PR) decreases sleep onset latency, increases total sleep time, reduces awakenings. Improves caregiver quality of life. Level of evidence often low (small/poor studies). Positive but Low Evidence
  • Neurodegenerative Diseases (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's):
    • Lower CSF melatonin levels observed.
    • Alzheimer's: Small studies suggest modest benefits on sleep/cognition (3-5mg IR or 2mg PR), but results are conflicting. Mixed/Conflicting
    • Parkinson's: Improves subjective sleep quality (small studies, variable doses). Particularly effective for REM Sleep Behavior Disorder (RBD). Positive (especially RBD)
  • Multiple Sclerosis (MS): 5mg melatonin improved sleep quality (but not fatigue) in one study. Lower inflammation markers noted. Mixed/Limited
  • Chronic Pain: Preclinical evidence for analgesic effect (MT2). Clinical evidence limited/mixed. Some studies show reduced post-op pain. Small studies suggest benefit in fibromyalgia (3-5mg) and cancer pain (3mg IR). Mixed/Limited
  • Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS): Symptoms often peak when melatonin is high. Exogenous melatonin may worsen RLS symptoms. Potential Negative Effect
  • Hypertension: Insomnia increases risk. PR melatonin (2-3mg) significantly decreased nocturnal blood pressure (systolic/diastolic) in meta-analysis; IR did not. No effect on daytime BP. Positive (PR for nocturnal BP)
  • Diabetes Mellitus: Reduced melatonin in diabetics (esp. with polyneuropathy). PR melatonin (Circadin 2mg) improved insomnia and HbA1c levels in one open-label study. Mixed/Limited (PR)

Melatonin for Circadian Rhythm Sleep-Wake Disorders

Based largely on the 2015 AASM Clinical Practice Guideline recommendations.

Falling asleep and waking >2 hours later than desired/conventional times.

  • Adults: Melatonin (0.3-5mg) improves sleep latency. Evidence limited (small studies). Recommendation: Low strength. Low Evidence/Rec
  • Children/Adolescents (no comorbidities): 0.05-0.15 mg/kg 1.5-2 hrs before bedtime showed modest improvement in sleep latency (one study). Low Evidence
  • Children/Adolescents (with psychiatric disorders): 3-5 mg IR between 6-7 PM showed advance in sleep onset (2 studies). Low Evidence
  • Timing: Crucial - typically administered several hours before desired bedtime or natural DLMO to advance the clock.

Falling asleep and waking several hours earlier than desired/conventional times.

  • No systematic studies on melatonin use.
  • Recommendation: Not recommended due to lack of evidence. Not Recommended

Sleep-wake cycle is not entrained to the 24-hour day, often drifts later. Common in totally blind individuals.

  • Observational studies (small samples) show melatonin (0.5-10 mg) administered 1 hr before bedtime or at fixed time (e.g. 9 PM) is effective in entraining the rhythm.
  • Recommendation: Effective, but based on low level of evidence. Effective (Low Evidence)

Chaotic, unpredictable sleep-wake pattern across 24 hours. Often seen in dementia or neurological disorders.

  • Elderly with Dementia: AASM does NOT recommend melatonin (based on one study of 6mg PR showing no benefit, potential risks). Not Recommended (Dementia)
  • Children/Adolescents with Neurological Disorders: One study showed benefit with 2-10 mg melatonin 1 hr before bed. Recommendation grade is low, evidence level moderate. Low/Moderate Evidence (Children/Neuro)

Conclusions & Key Takeaways

  • Melatonin plays a crucial physiological role in regulating the sleep-wake cycle, with levels naturally declining with age.
  • Exogenous melatonin can be useful for certain sleep disorders, but evidence varies significantly by condition, age group, and formulation.
  • Strongest Support:
    • Sleep-onset insomnia in children/adolescents (often related to DSWPD). Positive
    • Primary insomnia in adults >55 years (Prolonged-Release formulation). Positive (PR >55)
    • REM Sleep Behavior Disorder (RBD) in Parkinson's Disease. Positive (RBD)
    • Free-Running Disorder (N24SWD), especially in the blind. Positive
    • Insomnia in children with ASD/Neurodevelopmental disorders. Positive (Low Evidence)
  • Weaker / Insufficient / Mixed Evidence:
    • Primary insomnia in adults <55 years. Insufficient
    • Delayed Sleep-Wake Phase Disorder (DSWPD) in adults. Mixed/Low
    • Insomnia comorbid with depression, schizophrenia, Alzheimer's, pain. Mixed/Limited
  • Not Recommended / Potential Harm:
    • Advanced Sleep-Wake Phase Disorder (ASWPD). Negative
    • Irregular Sleep-Wake Rhythm in elderly with dementia. Negative
    • Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS). Potential Negative
  • Formulation Matters: Prolonged-release (PR) formulations better mimic physiological secretion and may be more effective for sleep maintenance and certain comorbidities (e.g., nocturnal hypertension, >55 insomnia). Immediate-release (IR) may be suited for sleep onset issues.
  • Safety: Melatonin generally has a good safety profile with few side effects reported in studies.
  • Important Note: Melatonin is a pharmacological agent, not just a nutritional supplement, and should be used judiciously, considering timing, dosage, and indication. More high-quality research is needed for many applications.
